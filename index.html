Overview of Global Carbon Emissions (tCO₂/person, MtCO₂, kgCO₂/GDP)

Carbon emissions play a critical role in shaping the trajectory of climate change. However, their sources and magnitudes are far from evenly distributed across the globe, resulting in stark disparities that reflect differences in economic development, population size, and energy use.

The Choropleth Map on the right provides a country-level view of carbon emissions intensity. By visualizing emissions per capita, per GDP, or total emissions, users can identify patterns that highlight high-emission regions, such as industrialized nations with large populations like China, the United States, and India. Meanwhile, smaller or less industrialized nations often have significantly lower emissions.

Complementing this, the Symbol Map offers a more granular perspective by using circles to represent emission volumes. The size and intensity of the circles reflect the magnitude of emissions, making it easy to spot major contributors and regional hotspots at a glance.

Together, these visualizations provide a layered understanding of carbon emissions across metrics and time. The interactive time slider enables users to explore trends over decades, showcasing the evolution of emissions and how global patterns have shifted in response to population growth, policy changes, and technological advancements. Use the Metric Selector to customize the view and uncover deeper insights into the global carbon footprint.

The journey of carbon emissions over time reflects economic shifts, policy decisions, and global awareness.

The line chart tracks emissions from the mid-20th century to the present, revealing distinct patterns among nations. The United States, once a leading emitter, peaked in the late 20th century before stabilizing due to advances in technology and environmental policies. Meanwhile, China’s rapid industrialization in the 21st century propelled it to the forefront of global emissions, with India following a similar upward trajectory. Filters allow a closer look at specific countries or metrics like per capita emissions, enabling a deeper exploration of the factors shaping these trends. Each curve in the chart speaks to the challenges and progress of individual nations.

Carbon emissions are heavily concentrated in a handful of countries, shaping the planet’s climate challenges.

This ranking chart identifies the top emitters across different metrics. Countries like Qatar and the United States lead in emissions per capita, reflecting their energy consumption and economic structure, while nations like China dominate in total emissions. Adjusting the number, metric or year uncovers shifting dynamics among nations, highlighting how industrial growth, resource dependence, and environmental policies influence their standings. This visualization captures the scale of responsibility carried by leading emitters and the pivotal role they play in mitigating climate change.

Americans have the highest per capita CO2 emissions out of anyone else in the world. On average, Americans produce 14.45 metric tons of CO2 annually, which is over 3 times the global per capita of 4.53 metric tons. Looking at this breakdown of the average American’s carbon footprint, we can see that transportation and housing make up over half of our yearly emissions. Americans have a strong reliance on personal car transportation, which, although improving with the adoption of electric vehicles, still requires the burning of a lot of fossil fuels. Some of these emissions we can directly control, like the direct fuel used for driving or the electricity used for keeping our homes warm in the winter. Others, like CO2 produced by our foods, are more indirect and result from the production and transportation of such goods.

The electronics industry plays a significant role in carbon emissions, from manufacturing to usage. This dashboard provides insights into the lifecycle emissions of electronic products, highlighting the impact of production processes and energy usage. Identifying the hotspots within this industry can pave the way for sustainable innovations.

Household activities, including energy use, waste management, and transportation, are significant contributors to global emissions. This visualization delves into the household sector, offering a detailed analysis of how lifestyle choices and consumption patterns impact the carbon footprint. Exploring this data can inspire impactful changes at an individual and community level.

This visualization examines the carbon footprints of various celebrities, focusing on their travel habits and lifestyle choices. By analyzing this data, we can shed light on the impact of high-profile individuals on climate change and explore ways they could set examples for a more sustainable future.

About the Data

Data and information in Overview of Global Carbon Emissions section is sourced from the Global Carbon Atlas, which provides carbon emissions data aggregated by country and region. For this part, three datasets from the source was processed and merged to highlight global emission trends using three metrics.
